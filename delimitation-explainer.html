India's Constitutional Crisis · April 2026

Delimitation · Parliament · South India

The Seats That Divided India

A fifty-year demographic bargain expires in 2026. The government's plan to resolve it just failed on the floor of Parliament — for the first time in twelve years, Modi lost a constitutional vote.

01 / Background

The Bargain Made in 1976

India's Constitution requires that seats in the Lok Sabha be allocated to states in proportion to their populations, and that constituency boundaries be redrawn after every census. It is a straightforward democratic principle: people are represented, not territories.

But in 1976, during the Emergency, the government made an exception. States across India were at very different stages of population growth. Southern states were already implementing family planning aggressively. Northern states were not. If representation followed population and northern states continued to grow faster, the south would simply bleed political power over the coming decades — a structural punishment for doing the right thing.

So Parliament froze it. The 42nd Constitutional Amendment locked each state's Lok Sabha seat count based on the 1971 census. This freeze was extended in 2001 by the 84th Amendment — this time until the first census published after 2026. The logic: give all states more time to bring fertility rates down, without fear of losing seats in the meantime.

That deadline is now. We have arrived at 2026.

Five Decades in Six Steps

1971
Last reallocation. Lok Sabha seats are last allocated to states based on actual population. 543 seats. This number and distribution stands to this day.
1976
The freeze. 42nd Amendment locks seat counts per state at 1971 levels. Boundaries could be redrawn inside states, but each state's total seats could not change.
2001
Extended. 84th Amendment extends the freeze to the "first census after 2026." A Delimitation Commission redraws constituency boundaries within states using 2001 data, but seat counts remain frozen at 1971 levels.
2023
Women's reservation passed. The Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam reserves one-third of Lok Sabha and assembly seats for women — but links its implementation to a future census and delimitation, leaving it effectively dormant.
Apr 16, 2026
The government moves. Three bills introduced in Lok Sabha: expand Parliament to 850 seats, use 2011 census for delimitation, and activate women's reservation.
Apr 17, 2026
The vote fails. Constitutional amendment falls short of the required two-thirds majority. Government withdraws the companion bills. First such defeat for Modi in 12 years.
02 / The Tension

The North-South Fault Line

The delimitation problem is, at its core, a story about demography and democracy pulling in opposite directions. Southern states — Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Telangana — invested heavily in education, healthcare, and family planning. They succeeded. Their populations stabilised. Their economies diversified.

Northern states, particularly Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, continued to grow rapidly. Their populations are substantially larger today than in 1971, relative to the south's.

A strict proportional reallocation of seats based on today's population would translate directly into a transfer of parliamentary power from south to north. States that spent fifty years doing exactly what the Constitution incentivised them to do would see their political voice diluted. States that did not would be rewarded with more seats.

This is not merely a technocratic objection. In Tamil Nadu especially, it has become a question of civilisational identity, federal dignity, and democratic fairness. M.K. Stalin and the DMK have made it an existential issue in the state's politics.

"They tried to divide us as North and South, to weaken and defeat us, and to redraw India's political map for their own gain. But INDIA stood together and defeated their design."

— M.K. Stalin, Chief Minister, Tamil Nadu, April 17, 2026

The current arrangement — still based on 1971 seat allocations, with 2001 constituency boundaries — is itself a compromise frozen in amber. The freeze worked as a pressure-release valve. But valves don't last forever.

03 / The Government's Plan

850 Seats and a Bigger Pie

The Modi government's solution was elegant in theory: don't shrink anyone's share, just make the Parliament much larger. By expanding the Lok Sabha from 543 to 850 seats, southern states could retain or gain seats in absolute numbers even if their proportional share declined.

Home Minister Amit Shah made this case explicitly in Parliament: with 543 seats today, southern states hold 129 seats (about 23.76%). Under the new proposal, the south would gain 66 additional seats in absolute terms even while the north gained proportionally more.

The three-bill package introduced on April 16 did the following:

Lok Sabha Seats — Now

543

Allocation based on 1971 census. Unchanged for 55 years.

Proposed Maximum

850

815 from States + 35 from Union Territories.

Census Proposed for Use

2011

Rather than waiting for the ongoing 2027 census.

Women's Reservation

33%

To be activated immediately via this delimitation, not tied to future census.

The government also activated the dormant 2023 Women's Reservation Act on April 16 — the day before the vote — notifying it into force from that date. This was a deliberate framing move: the vote was being cast as being about women's rights, not just reapportionment.

The opposition's counterargument was sharp. Congress argued that women's reservation should simply be applied to the existing 543 seats immediately — no delimitation required. Why link the two at all?

04 / The Vote

The First Constitutional Defeat in Twelve Years

Constitutional amendments require a two-thirds majority of members present and voting in the Lok Sabha. This is a higher bar than ordinary legislation, by design. It prevents narrow majorities from rewriting foundational law.

When the vote came on April 17, the government fell short. Out of 528 members present and voting, 298 supported the bill and 230 voted against. The bill needed roughly 352 votes. It got 298.

Following the defeat of the constitutional amendment, Law Minister Kiren Rijiju withdrew the companion Delimitation Bill and the Union Territories Laws (Amendment) Bill. The entire package collapsed.

This was the first time in twelve years that the Modi government failed to pass a constitutional amendment — a significant political moment, not only substantively but symbolically. The INDIA alliance, which has often struggled with cohesion, held together on this vote.

"The attempt to expand the Lok Sabha to 850 seats and push delimitation based on the 2011 Census raised serious concerns about fairness and balance. The NDA government is clearly on borrowed time."

— Abhishek Banerjee, MP, Trinamool Congress
05 / What Happens Now

A Deadline Passed, a Problem Unsolved

The State of Play — April 2026

The freeze has legally expired. The 84th Amendment's protection lapses in 2026. Nothing has replaced it. India is in constitutional limbo.

Delimitation cannot happen immediately. Without the constitutional amendment, the government cannot use the 2011 census as the basis for a new delimitation. The 2027 census data doesn't exist yet.

Women's reservation is notified but hollow. The 2023 Act is now formally in force as of April 16, 2026 — but since it requires a delimitation to operationalise seat reservations, it remains practically unimplemented. The 2029 elections are likely to proceed without it.

The government may return with a revised package. A renegotiated formula — one that offers stronger constitutional guarantees to southern states — is the most likely path forward. But that requires opposition buy-in.

The 2027 census is underway. Its reference date is March 1, 2027. Once published, it becomes the constitutionally mandated basis for delimitation — making the question of which census to use moot, but also delaying any resolution well beyond 2029.

The deeper truth is that the defeat of this bill solved nothing. It deferred a genuinely difficult constitutional problem that the original architects of the freeze knew would have to be confronted eventually. The question of how to honour democratic proportionality while not punishing states for their own governance successes has no easy answer.

India has now passed through the 2026 deadline without a resolution. The next census will create new facts on the ground. And the next government will inherit a problem that has only gotten harder with each passing year of inaction.

06 / The Larger Stakes

Federalism, Fairness, and the Shape of Indian Democracy

Delimitation is, at one level, a technical exercise: draw boundaries, count people, allocate seats. But underneath it is a foundational question about what kind of federal democracy India wants to be.

A Parliament where every vote is equal regardless of which state it is cast in is a perfectly legitimate democratic ideal. A Parliament where states are protected from being overwhelmed by the demographic weight of their neighbours — and where good governance is not structurally penalised — is also a legitimate ideal. These ideals are in tension here, and that tension does not resolve itself.

Southern states look at this debate and see a slow-motion erosion of their influence over national policy — on language, on fiscal transfers, on development priorities. The seats question is inseparable from those anxieties. Delimitation is not just about Parliament. It is about who India belongs to, and who gets to decide.

That question will not be settled by one vote in the Lok Sabha. But what happened on April 17, 2026 was a signal — that the south will not go quietly, and that any durable solution will have to earn their consent, not override it.
